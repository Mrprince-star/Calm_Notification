The Story Behind Calm Notifications

Calm Notifications wasn't built by a company. It was built by one person, entirely on an Android phone, without a computer, without a team, and without a budget — because the problem it solves was a real, everyday frustration, not a market opportunity spotted on a spreadsheet.

Starting with the problem, not the app

Before writing a single line of code, the actual research came first: reading through real complaints on Reddit, forums, and everyday conversations about phones buzzing constantly, about muting one app at a time never quite working, about missing something important the one time everything got silenced. Notification overload kept coming up as a genuine, unsolved daily annoyance — not a niche complaint, an almost universal one.

Built entirely from a phone

There was no laptop, no desktop, no traditional development setup. Every single screen, every line of Kotlin and Dart, every bug fix in this app was written and tested from an Android phone using a cloud-based development environment. If that sounds like an unusual way to build a real, working Android app — it is. But it's also proof that the barrier to building something useful has never been lower, even without expensive equipment.

Privacy as a starting principle, not an afterthought

From the very first version, one rule was non-negotiable: Calm Notifications would never read the actual content of a notification — only which app it came from. No message text, no titles, ever stored or transmitted. That constraint shaped every feature decision since, including turning down fabricated "engagement" metrics and gamified statistics that sounded impressive but weren't honest.

Who's behind it

Calm Notifications is built and maintained by Prince, an independent, self-taught developer based in Nigeria, under the FaithfulBiz name. No prior formal programming background — just a real problem worth solving properly, and the persistence to learn what was needed along the way.

What's next

Calm Notifications is the first app under FaithfulBiz, with more planned. The approach stays the same regardless of what gets built next: start from a real problem, respect people's privacy by default, and build something honest rather than something merely optimized to look impressive.
